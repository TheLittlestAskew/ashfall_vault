
The Twin Goddesses’ Daggers: Emberkiss and Frostfall Forged in the dawn years of Britannia, the twin blades were gifts from the Sister Goddesses of Fire and Ice, Astraea Ignis and Astraea Glacia. Though opposites, the sisters shared one purpose: to arm a mortal champion capable of restoring balance whenever either element threatened to swallow the world. Emberkiss (Fire Dagger)  
Born from a shard of the first star to touch the world, Emberkiss burns with a constant ember‑glow along its edge. Its flame never consumes the wielder but sears anything it strikes. Wounds made by Emberkiss smolder long after the blade withdraws, marking the victim with thin trails of glowing ash. Frostfall (Ice Dagger)  
Frostfall was carved from the heart of an ancient glacier said to predate humanity. Its blade is impossibly cold, rimed with pale blue runes that breathe mist. A cut from Frostfall spreads numbing cold through flesh and bone, slowing movement as frost crystallizes in the wound. Twin-Bound Nature  
When wielded together, the daggers resonate a faint hum like distant stormwinds. Their combined presence stabilizes volatile magic: fire doesn’t run wild, and ice doesn’t deaden the world around it. Separated too long, each grows more extreme: Emberkiss burns hotter, Frostfall freezes deeper, and the sisters’ balance begins to strain

[[[Samothy Smith-Wesson]]] this is yours: Chapter I — Origins of the Vampire Rule

In the last years of the Withering Century, when famine hollowed the lands and plague thinned the living to whispers, thirteen mortals sought deliverance beyond the reach of gods. They were scholars, warlords, healers, and heretics—united only by desperation. In the ruins beneath Hollow Dawn Keep, they found the remnant of a night‑spirit whose name had long been scoured from divine record.

What passed between them cannot be known with certainty. Some accounts claim they begged for power to save their people; others insist they craved dominion. But all agree on the bargain’s price. The spirit granted them immortality unbound by age, strength beyond mortal measure, and mastery over the life‑essence of all living things. In return, it cursed them with undying hunger and tethered their souls to an eternal night no sunrise could break.

Their transformation marked the first rising of the coven that would be known as The Thirteen of the Hollow Dawn. Their blood thickened with shadow, their eyes dimmed to embers, and their hearts beat only when they fed. Those granted the tainted gift of their blood rose as lesser vampires—servants, soldiers, and courtiers of a new order.

The world, fractured by starvation and sickness, scarcely resisted. Villages surrendered to the Thirteen, believing any rule preferable to the chaos that had devoured their kin. Some whispered that the new lords brought protection, quelling raiders and bandits with effortless cruelty. Others welcomed them as saviors who never slept and never tired.

By the time the truth of the curse became clear—when the disappearances began, and the night grew thick with whispers of thirst—the Thirteen had already carved their dominion across the land. Fear replaced gratitude, but fear could not undo what had already taken root.

Thus began the first nights of the Vampire Rule, born not from conquest, but from a desperate plea in a dying age.


[[Barrett Grimmskar]] Chapter 1: The Gift of Dawn Before the wars, before the rise of the blood-born tyrants, Britannia knew an age so radiant that later generations would speak of it as a dream. Its beginning was marked not by the crowning of a king, but by the descent of a goddess. On the longest day of the solstice, the Goddess of the Sun Aurona, She Who Rises stepped from a pillar of living light onto the cliffs of Highreach. Witnesses described her arrival as a sunrise given human form: hair like burning gold, eyes bright enough to cast shadows, her presence warming the stone beneath her feet. Word spread across the realm in an instant, carried on both wind and prayer. In her hands she bore a single fragment of concentrated daylight: the Sun Shard, still molten at its core, pulsing with a heartbeat that was not entirely its own. She offered it freely to the people of Britannia, declaring it a covenant between the mortal realm and the eternal flame. As long as it was honored, the land would flourish. And flourish it did. Fields once battered by storms grew in perfect cycles. Fruit ripened with impossible sweetness. Winters softened. Plague retreated. For a hundred years, the Shard’s radiance flowed through the realm’s temples and watchtowers, carried by solar priests who learned to channel its warmth without being consumed by it. Trade expanded, populations swelled, and art flourished in a golden age unmatched before or since. The people believed the prosperity would last forever, safeguarded by divine favor. They did not yet understand that the brighter the light, the darker the shadow it casts—and that far beyond the horizon, enemies who could not abide the sun had already taken notice.
