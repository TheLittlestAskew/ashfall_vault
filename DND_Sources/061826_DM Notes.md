
# **Chapter 2 of The Sun Shard book: The Gathering Dusk**

Britannia knew peace and prosperity for hundreds of years. Under the Sun Shard’s eternal radiance, the land blossomed in ways that seemed impossible in the ages before. Crops grew tall and golden, rivers ran clear even in winter, and sickness became a fading memory spoken of only in old stories. The people believed the light would never dim.

At the heart of this golden age stood the **Church of the First Dawn**, a sanctuary built around the Sun Shard itself. Within its marble halls, the monks of the **Order of the Sun** tended to the sacred relic with unwavering devotion. They rose with the dawn, chanting prayers that resonated like warm bells through the city streets, and they slept only when the Shard’s glow softened to a gentle ember at night.

Pilgrims traveled from every corner of Britannia to stand before the Shard’s altar. Some came seeking healing. Others came for blessings, guidance, or simply to bask in the warmth that felt like the goddess’s own breath. Offerings of flowers, carved tokens, and handwritten prayers piled at the foot of the altar until the monks cleared them each evening, only for the mound to return by morning.

The Shard answered many of those prayers.

Fields blighted by drought found new life. Children born weak grew strong. Entire villages were spared from storms that should have torn them apart. The people saw these miracles and believed the covenant with Aurona would protect them forever.

But peace has a way of softening vigilance.

While Britannia celebrated its golden age, **something else stirred beyond the reach of the sun**. In forgotten corners of the world — in caverns where daylight had never touched, in ruins swallowed by night, in kingdoms that had long since turned their backs on the goddess — a different power was gathering strength.

It watched Britannia’s prosperity with growing hatred. It felt the Shard’s warmth like a blade pressed against its throat. And it whispered to those who still remembered the old darkness, promising them a world remade in shadow.

The people of Britannia did not see the signs. They did not notice the nights growing a little colder, or the way the dawn sometimes hesitated on the horizon. They did not question why the monks’ prayers grew longer each year, or why the Order of the Sun began doubling their vigils.

They believed the light would last forever.

They did not yet understand that **every age of light casts a deeper shadow** — and that in those shadows, a force of ancient malice was rising. A force that would shatter their peace, scorch their fields, and birth a new world from ash.

The golden age was ending.

And none of them knew it.
